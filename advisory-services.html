When Leaders Bring Monderman In

The need is usually felt before it is fully named.

Institutions rarely describe the problem in structural terms at first. More often, it is experienced as recurring friction, stalled movement, or a growing sense that the system is no longer producing what leadership intends.

Decisions are made — but stall, loop, or degrade before they reach execution.

What appears to be slowness is often a symptom of overloaded pathways, diffused authority, or governance that no longer fits the work.

Strategy is clear at the top, but fragments as it moves through the organization.

The problem is not usually intent. It is the system through which interpretation, ownership, and action are expected to hold together.

Accountability exists in theory, but diffuses in practice.

Roles appear defined, yet responsibility becomes blurred as work crosses teams, approvals, and institutional layers.

Teams are working harder, but the system produces less.

Increased effort can temporarily mask structural drag, but it cannot overcome a design that steadily absorbs time, clarity, and decision energy.

Administrative processes continue to expand, even as performance slows.

More controls, more coordination, and more procedural density often signal a system compensating for lost coherence rather than restoring it.

The same issues resurface — despite repeated intervention.

When friction is systemic, local fixes rarely hold. The visible problem changes shape, but the underlying structure remains untouched.
