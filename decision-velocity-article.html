Our Approach

Decision Velocity

Most decisions don’t fail—they stall. Decision velocity is the organization’s ability to move from intent to action without unnecessary delay, rework, or procedural drag.

Most organizations do not have a decision problem in the abstract. They have a decision-speed problem in practice. Leaders can usually see what needs attention. Teams often know what should happen next. The breakdown occurs in the middle—where approvals, reviews, handoffs, unclear ownership, and the quiet fear of acting without cover begin to slow movement.

That is what decision velocity is really about. Not speed for its own sake, but the ability to move from signal to decision to action before the environment has already changed.

Decision velocity is not recklessness. It is the disciplined ability to make timely decisions at the clock speed of reality.

Why good organizations still move too slowly

Delay is rarely caused by one dramatic bottleneck. More often, it is the combined effect of many small frictions: one more review, one more stakeholder, one more committee, one more request for a slide that will not change the outcome. Over time, these accumulate into a system that feels careful but behaves slowly.

This matters because delay changes the economics of judgment. A good decision made too late is often indistinguishable from a bad one. By the time approval is granted, the customer has moved, the competitor has acted, or the internal window for action has closed.

The hidden cost of slow decisions

Teams stop escalating ideas

When people learn that worthwhile ideas spend months in review, they begin to self-filter before the organization ever sees the signal.

Leaders become overloaded

Unclear decision rights push too many choices upward. Senior leaders become the system’s workaround.

Execution loses tempo

Even strong teams struggle when decisions land late, arrive half-settled, or keep being reopened.

Risk actually increases

Slow organizations often believe they are being safer. In reality, they may simply be allowing unresolved issues to age inside the system.

What higher decision velocity looks like

Faster decisions do not begin with motivational slogans. They begin with cleaner design. Decision rights need to sit where the relevant information is. Approval thresholds need to match actual risk. Review paths need to be visible. Exceptions should be rare, not the normal way work moves.

In healthier systems, people know which decisions are reversible and can move quickly, and which are consequential enough to require deeper review. That distinction alone can dramatically improve tempo.

How Monderman approaches decision velocity

We look at the full pathway beneath the decision, not just the moment of choice. Where is work waiting? Where is authority fragmented? Which approvals are performing real governance, and which are simply inherited habits? Where has the organization become dependent on escalation instead of coherent design?

The goal is not permanent acceleration. It is more intelligent movement. Some decisions should be slower. Many should be faster. The real task is to design a system that knows the difference.

Common signs decision velocity is degrading

Deadlines slip while activity stays high. More decisions are pushed upward. Teams rely on personal relationships to get things unstuck. “Urgent” becomes the only way anything moves.

What this affects

Speed, trust, and adaptability

Slow decisions do not just reduce tempo. They train people to become passive, political, or overly cautious over time.

Look for

  • Approval cycles longer than the problem’s shelf life
  • Escalation becoming routine
  • Decisions reopened after sign-off
  • Action waiting on presentation-quality polish

Monderman view

Decision velocity is not about haste. It is about keeping judgment, authority, and timing aligned closely enough that organizations can still maneuver.
