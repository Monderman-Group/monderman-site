Our Approach

Operational Systems

Processes expand over time—often invisibly. Operational systems determine whether coordination, approvals, and workflows enable execution or quietly slow it down.

Every organization has operational systems: workflows, reporting structures, compliance routines, controls, templates, approvals, and documentation requirements that shape how work actually moves. These systems are often treated as neutral background infrastructure. They are not.

Operational systems determine whether effort compounds or gets absorbed. They shape how long work waits, how often people must stop and translate what they are doing into administrative proof, and how much of the day is spent producing value versus maintaining the machinery around value.

The question is not whether your organization has operational systems. The question is whether those systems are helping work move or quietly competing with the work itself.

How good systems become heavy systems

Most burdensome systems did not begin as nonsense. A report was added to improve visibility. A control was added after a failure. A review step was introduced to reduce risk. Over time, though, few of these mechanisms are revisited with the same seriousness that introduced them.

The result is accumulation. One team is maintaining yesterday’s safeguard. Another is complying with a template no one fully trusts. A third is spending hours preparing information that is already available somewhere else. The organization grows, but its operating burden grows with it.

What operational drag looks like in practice

Duplicate reporting

The same information is prepared multiple times for different audiences or systems, each with slightly different formats and expectations.

Review chains with unclear value

Work passes through several hands, but the additional quality, risk reduction, or insight is hard to detect.

Compliance that outlives the original risk

Controls remain in place long after the surrounding conditions have changed.

Frontline capacity loss

Skilled employees spend more of their time proving the work than doing the work.

Operational systems should create flow, not friction

Healthy systems do not mean minimal systems. In many organizations, strong controls are necessary. The issue is fitness, not ideology. A strong operational system is one that remains intelligible, proportionate, and responsive. It supports the mission rather than quietly feeding on it.

In practice, that means periodic burden audits, pruning redundant processes, aligning controls to actual risk, and redesigning handoffs so information does not have to be constantly repackaged just to cross internal boundaries.

How Monderman approaches operational systems

We examine the operating burden beneath the org chart: where documentation is expanding, where approvals are stacking, where reporting is duplicative, and where people closest to the work are being converted into custodians of the system instead of contributors to the mission.

The aim is not simplistic streamlining. It is to return capacity to the organization. Time, attention, and judgment are finite resources. Every unnecessary layer of process spends them.

Common signs your operational systems need redesign

Teams spend too much time updating trackers, preparing slides, re-entering data, or waiting for routine approvals. People know the system feels heavy, but no one owns the burden as a system-level issue.

Monderman view
